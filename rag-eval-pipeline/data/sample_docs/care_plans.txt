PATIENT CARE PLAN DOCUMENTATION
General Guidelines for Common Conditions

TYPE 2 DIABETES MANAGEMENT

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic condition where the body does not use insulin effectively. Management focuses on keeping blood glucose levels within a target range to prevent complications.

Diet: Patients should follow a balanced diet low in refined sugars and processed carbohydrates. Aim for consistent meal timing to help regulate blood sugar. A dietitian referral is recommended for all newly diagnosed patients.

Exercise: Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week such as walking, swimming, or cycling. Exercise helps the body use insulin more effectively and can lower blood glucose levels.

Medication: Metformin is commonly the first-line medication and is typically taken with meals to reduce gastrointestinal side effects. Blood glucose monitoring should be done as advised by your physician.

Monitoring: Regular HbA1c tests (every 3 months initially, then every 6 months if stable) measure average blood sugar over the past 3 months. Target HbA1c is typically below 7% for most adults.

Complications to watch for: numbness or tingling in feet, blurred vision, slow-healing wounds, or frequent infections. Report these to your physician promptly.

HYPERTENSION (HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE) MANAGEMENT

Hypertension occurs when blood pressure is consistently above 130/80 mmHg. It often has no symptoms but significantly increases risk of heart disease and stroke.

Lifestyle: Reduce sodium intake to less than 2,300mg per day. Follow the DASH diet (rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy). Limit alcohol consumption. Avoid smoking.

Exercise: Regular aerobic exercise (30 minutes most days) can lower blood pressure by 5 to 8 mmHg. Consistency is more important than intensity.

Medication: ACE inhibitors like Lisinopril help relax blood vessels. Take as prescribed, usually once daily. Do not stop taking medication without consulting your physician even if blood pressure readings improve.

Monitoring: Home blood pressure monitoring is encouraged. Take readings at the same time each day. Bring your log to appointments.

HIGH CHOLESTEROL MANAGEMENT

High cholesterol increases the risk of cardiovascular disease. Statins like Atorvastatin are commonly prescribed to reduce LDL (bad) cholesterol.

Diet: Limit saturated fats found in red meat and full-fat dairy. Increase fibre intake through oats, beans, and fruits. Omega-3 fatty acids from fish like salmon and mackerel are beneficial.

Statins: Take Atorvastatin at bedtime as cholesterol production is highest at night. Report any muscle pain, weakness, or dark urine to your physician as these may be side effects requiring attention.

Monitoring: Lipid panel blood tests are recommended every 6 to 12 months. Target LDL is typically below 100 mg/dL for most patients and below 70 mg/dL for high-risk patients.
